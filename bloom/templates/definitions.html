Understanding Bloom's Taxonomy, Course Outcomes, and Program Outcomes

What is Bloom's Taxonomy?

Bloom's Taxonomy is a hierarchical framework used to classify educational learning objectives into levels of complexity and specificity. It helps instructors design, assess, and categorize learning activities based on cognitive skills.

The six levels of Bloom's Taxonomy (revised version) are:

  • Remembering: Recalling facts and basic concepts.
  • Understanding: Explaining ideas or concepts.
  • Applying: Using information in new situations.
  • Analyzing: Breaking information into parts to explore relationships.
  • Evaluating: Justifying a decision or course of action.
  • Creating: Producing new or original work.

What are Course Outcomes (COs)?

Course Outcomes (COs) are specific statements that define what a student is expected to achieve at the end of a course. COs are measurable and align with Program Outcomes (POs).

Examples of Course Outcomes:

  • "At the end of this course, students will be able to analyze sorting algorithms."
  • "Students will design and implement database schemas."

What are Program Outcomes (POs)?

Program Outcomes (POs) are broad statements that describe the knowledge, skills, and attitudes students should have acquired by the time they graduate from a program. These are defined by national or institutional accreditation bodies and often align with industry requirements.

Examples of Program Outcomes:

  • "Ability to apply engineering principles to solve complex problems."
  • "Demonstrate professional ethics and responsibility in practice."
